TB MINI RAG DOCUMENT (GROUNDING)
GENERAL FACTS ABOUT TB
- TB disease is one of the world’s leading infectious disease killers.
- Anyone can get TB.
- TB is an infection, most commonly of the lungs, caused by the bacterium Mycobacterium tuberculosis.
- Spread through the air from person to person.
- TB bacteria can stay in the air for several hours in some environments.
- TB is treatable with antibiotics.
- Multi-drug-resistant strains of the TB bacteria can make treatment more difficult, so adherence to antibiotic therapy is very important.

SIGNS AND SYMPTOMS
- Cough lasting three weeks or more
- Chest pain
- Coughing up blood or sputum (phlegm from deep in the lungs)
- Weakness or fatigue
- Weight loss (particularly without a known cause)
- Loss of appetite
- Chills
- Fever
- Night Sweats

DIAGNOSIS
- Individuals at increased risk of having TB or are suspected of having TB infection undergo testing. There are typically two steps to testing:
- Step one: Skin Test or Blood Testing
  - The TB Skin Test:
    - A healthcare provider puts some testing material under the skin using a small needle
    - The individual being tested returns to the healthcare provider in two to three days to determine if there is a reaction.
    - If a reaction is present, then further testing is needed to determine if the individual has an active case of TB infection.
  - The TB Blood Test (also called an interferon-gamma release assay or IGRA), is the appropriate testing method for individuals who have ever been vaccinated for TB.
  - The TB blood test measures how the individual’s immune system reacts to the germs that cause TB.
  - If the blood test is positive, then further testing is needed to confirm active TB infection.
- Step Two: X-Ray and Sputum Testing
  - A Chest X-ray will show a TB infection in the lungs.
  - Sputum (phlegm) produced with coughing is tested for the presence of the TB bacteria.
  - A positive sputum test is confirmation of an active case of TB and that the person is currently able to spread the bacteria to others.

TREATMENT
- TB treatment can take four, six, or nine months depending on the treatment plan and typically involves taking multiple antibiotics.
- Sputum (phlegm) testing is done during treatment to ensure that the antibiotics are working, and the person is no longer infectious. This testing is done at different intervals depending on the treatment plan by the healthcare provider.
- Most Common Drug Therapies include:
  - Isoniazid
  - Rifampin
  - Ethambutol
  - Moxifloxacin
  - Rifapentine
  - Pyrazinamide
- Keep all medicine out of reach of children
- Take all TB medicines exactly as prescribed
  - Don’t miss any doses and do not stop treatment early.
  - It can be very dangerous to stop taking your medicines or not to take all your medicines regularly

- Common side effects (if you experience any of these, continue to take your medication):
  - Skin rash
  - Upset stomach
  - Nausea
  - Rifampin or rifapentine can cause some body fluids to turn an orange color. This is normal and harmless. The color fades over time. These body fluids can include:
    - Urine (pee)
    - Saliva
    - Tears
    - Sweat
    - Breastmilk
  - If you experience any discoloration of body fluids due to rifampin or rifapentine, a healthcare provider might recommend avoiding soft contact lenses to prevent permanent staining.

- Serious side effects (if you experience any of these symptoms, call your healthcare provider immediately):
  - Liver injury:
    - Abdominal pain
    - Nausea and vomiting
    - Skin and eyes turning yellow (jaundice)
  - Dizziness or lightheadedness
  - Loss of appetite
  - Flu-like symptoms
  - Tingling or numbness in your hands or feet
    - If you are taking isoniazid, you may experience tingling or numbness in your hands and feet. A healthcare provider may add vitamin B6 to prevent this.

- Treatment involves taking medicine by directly observed therapy (DOT), on your own, or a combination of both.
- For directly observed therapy (DOT): you will meet with a healthcare worker every day or several times a week in-person or virtually (through a smartphone, tablet, or computer).
  - They will watch you take your TB medications
  - They will monitor your progress in treatment to make sure that the medications are working as they should

- Tips for taking medicines on your own (use only if the user is asking about adherence/missed doses):
  - Take your pills at the same time every day (e.g., before breakfast, during a regular coffee break, or after brushing your teeth).
  - Set an alarm.
  - Write a note and place it somewhere visible.
  - Ask a family member or friend to remind you.
  - Mark off each day on a calendar; note symptoms.
  - Use a weekly pill dispenser.

PREVENTION
- TB is more likely to spread indoors or in places with poor air circulation (e.g., closed vehicle). Outdoor areas or areas with good air circulation have a lower risk.
- People with TB disease are most likely to spread TB germs to people they spend time with every day.
- Completing treatment as prescribed can prevent further spread to loved ones or the community.
